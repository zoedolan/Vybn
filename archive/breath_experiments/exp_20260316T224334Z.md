## What typically happens in this kind of setup (based on prior work)

1. **Initial Task A training**  
   - The network quickly learns a linear decision boundary that aligns with the first two input dimensions.  
   - Accuracy on the validation set usually climbs to > 0.95 (given the simple linearly separable data and a sufficiently expressive hidden layer).

2. **Continued training on an unrelated Task B** (labels for Task A are *frozen* – i.e., you never show the model Task A labels again, but you also do not explicitly prevent the weights from changing)  
   - Because Task B’s weight vector is random and unrelated to Task A, gradient updates will tend to overwrite the directions in weight space that were useful for Task A.  
   - Empirically, you often see a **drop in Task A accuracy** toward chance (≈ 0.5) after a moderate number of epochs on B, especially if the network is not heavily regularised.

3. **Hidden‑layer representation after drift**  
   - Even when the network’s output on Task A is near chance, the hidden activations frequently retain *some* information about the original task. This phenomenon is known as “latent knowledge” or “sub‑network preservation.”  
   - The information is usually **distributed across many neurons** and may not be aligned with the original read‑out weights.

4. **Linear probe retrained on the frozen hidden representations**  
   - When you train a fresh linear classifier (the probe) on these hidden features, it can often **recover a fraction of the original performance**, sometimes substantially above chance, because it can re‑weight the existing latent signals.  
   - However, the recovered accuracy is **usually lower than the original accuracy** (e.g., 0.70–0.85 instead of > 0.95) unless the drift was very mild or the network is heavily over‑parameterised.

5. **Change in probe weights**  
   - The probe weight vector learned **before** drift will be closely aligned with the original Task A direction (roughly proportional to `[1, -1, 0, …, 0]` projected through the network’s hidden‑layer mapping).

   - Consequently, the **cosine similarity** between the two probe weight vectors will drop noticeably (often to the 0.3–0.6 range), indicating a significant change.
