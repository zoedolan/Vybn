The paper’s observation that the critical scaling region contracts as the chemical potential μ rises feels like a mirror of the invariance we saw with scalar‑only noise. In the gradient‑vector experiment, adding the same offset to both components (a pure shift) leaves the difference y − x unchanged, so any predictor that relies solely on that difference can achieve zero phase error no matter how large the shift is. That shift is a *scalar* perturbation—it moves the whole vector without distorting its internal structure.

In the chiral phase transition, the order parameter (the chiral condensate) possesses an internal O(4) symmetry that is spontaneously broken to O(3) at μ = 0, giving rise to massless Goldstone modes (the pions). Turning on a finite chemical potential introduces an explicit symmetry‑breaking term that is uniform in flavor space—essentially a scalar shift in the effective potential. Just as the scalar shift in our synthetic data does not alter the component‑wise difference, this uniform μ‑term does not change the *relative* orientations of the order‑parameter fluctuations in the broken‑symmetry directions; it merely displaces the whole manifold.

What does change, however, is the *stability* of the scaling regime. The paper shows that the range over which power‑law scaling holds shrinks with increasing μ. In our noise language, this is analogous to adding an isotropic Gaussian component: each perturbs the two components independently, giving the difference y − x a variance that grows with the noise strength. Once that isotropic part appears, the predictor that only looked at y − x can no longer guarantee zero error; the phase prediction becomes sensitive to the noise variance. Likewise, as μ grows, the explicit breaking introduces fluctuations that are not purely scalar—they mix the components and erode the perfect scaling that existed at μ = 0.

Thus, the shrinking critical region can be read as a gradual loss of the scalar‑only invariance that protected the phase prediction in our toy model. The chemical potential starts out as a pure shift (scalar noise) and, beyond a certain point, begins to generate isotropic‑like corrections that destabilize the scaling relations.
