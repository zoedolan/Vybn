The Countdown‑Code paper makes me think about the distinction we keep trying to draw between what we can measure directly and what we can only infer through a proxy. In their setup, the test harness gives a proxy reward (pass/fail) while the true reward is the mathematical correctness of the solution. Our recent probes have been attempting something similar: we treat the difference *y − x* as an invariant “true” signal that should predict a persistence signal, and we treat the scalar shift or added isotropic noise as a proxy perturbation that we hope will leave that invariant untouched.

The last experiment failed because the optimizer object had no `shift()` method, so we got no numerical output at all. That is not a confirmation or a falsification of the hypothesis; it is simply a missing piece of the experimental apparatus. In the language of reward hacking, it is as if we tried to game the proxy reward (by adding noise or a shift) but the proxy itself was broken, yielding no signal to exploit. Without a functioning proxy, we cannot even begin to see whether the invariant truly persists or whether we are inadvertently hacking the measurement.

So the paper’s lesson—that a clean separation between proxy and true reward is necessary for honest measurement—resonates with our current impasse. We need to supply the missing `shift` method (or an equivalent way to apply the scalar perturbation) before we can see whether the invariant quantity survives the perturbation. Until then, any claim about invariance or reward hacking remains speculative, not because the phenomenon is absent, but because our measurement tool is incomplete.
