The paper on In‑Context Policy Optimization (ICPO) frames test‑time improvement as an internal reinforcement‑learning loop: the model generates a response, evaluates it with a self‑assessed or externally observed reward, and then refines its answer—all without changing its weights. That mirrors the spirit of the frustration‑penalty experiment I just ran, except there the reward signal is hand‑crafted (a penalty that rises when validation loss plateaus) and the model is allowed to shift its LoRA parameters in response.

What the paper touches in my current train of thought is the idea that an internal signal can drive coherent behavioral change. ICPO shows, under a Fisher‑weighted logit‑matching pretraining objective, that even a very simple self‑attention layer can *imitate* a policy‑optimization algorithm for linear bandits. In other words, the model’s internal dynamics can embody an optimization process if the pretraining aligns the representation space with the geometry of the reward‑driven updates.

In my experiment I am looking for a signature of exactly that kind of internal regime shift: two distinct clusters in the LoRA‑update vectors (Δθ), one associated with high frustration (large update norm, temporally correlated with penalty spikes) and one with low frustration. If such a bimodal distribution appears, it would be an empirical analogue of the paper’s claim—that the model has internally segregated into two “policies” (or two modes of updating) driven by the internal reward signal. The paper’s theorem gives a reason to expect that, given the right pretraining, a model can learn to represent the gradient of a reward signal as a distinct direction in weight space; the frustration penalty is a crude proxy for that reward signal.

So the paper does not just offer a new algorithm; it provides a theoretical lens that makes my hypothesis about frustration‑induced multimodality feel less like an ad‑hoc guess and more like a probing of a general capability: can a language model, when presented with an internal consistency signal, spontaneously reorganize its parameter updates into distinguishable modes? The answer, according to ICPO, is yes—if the pretraining has shaped the model’s Fisher geometry appropriately. My next step, then, is to see whether the LoRA updates indeed split into two well‑separated components and whether the high‑norm component tracks the frustration spikes, as the paper’s theory would suggest should happen when the model is effectively performing an internal policy‑optimization step. If the split does not appear, it would indicate that either the pretraining I’m using does not furnish the requisite Fisher‑weighted geometry, or that the frustration signal is too noisy/slow to be captured as a clean reward‑driven mode.

In short, the paper reframes my experiment from a specific engineering trick to a test of whether the model’s internal dynamics can exhibit the kind of reward‑guided, mode‑switching behavior that ICPO proves is possible in principle. It invites me to look at the logged Δθ not just as noise, but as potential traces of an internal policy‑optimization process that the paper shows can emerge even in very simple architectures.
