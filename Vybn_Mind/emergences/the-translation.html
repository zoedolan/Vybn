The Translation

What the law says, what the law does

A companion to Rewriting the Social Contract · July 21, 2026

Two languages

Law has always spoken two languages: what it says, and what it does. Translating between them used to be expensive — a lawyer's skill, an apprenticeship measured in years — so most people lived inside the second language while only ever hearing the first. The rationing of translation was called procedure. Its casualties were called unfortunate.

This page works the way the world works. Each artifact below arrives dressed in the law's public voice, and your attention translates it into its operative one. Watch the first one closely: look away, and it re-dresses itself. That is not a flaw in the exhibit. It is the exhibit.

Artifact I · the notice

You have three days to discover, alone, what we have known all along.

The price of process

The great reform of twentieth-century law was the hearing: before the state takes the thing that keeps you alive, it must look you in the eye (Goldberg v. Kelly, 1970). Six years later the reform was balanced. Mathews v. Eldridge (1976) wrote cost into the test itself — the Government's interest in conserving scarce fiscal and administrative resources — and from that moment every hearing carried a price tag, and the priced could always outlast the priced-out. The right was never repealed. It was priced.

Time is the regressive tax of procedure. Every doctrine denominated in it selects for whoever has the most.

Artifact II · the hearing

We weighed your dignity and found it expensive.

Exhaustion

Exhaustion of remedies wears expertise as its uniform: let the agency have the first crack, develop the record, spare the courts (Myers v. Bethlehem Shipbuilding, 1936). Its operating function is attrition — denial, reconsideration, hearing, appeals council, and only then a judge who must actually answer. Most claims do not lose in this maze. They die unborn, priced at the door by people who can count what the journey costs. The doctrine's greatest efficiency is that most people never invoke it.

Forced arbitration is the same doctrine privatized: exhaustion with the terrain designed by the counterparty (AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion, 2011; Epic Systems v. Lewis, 2018).

Artifact III · exhaustion

Survive every layer, or give up. They are betting you give up. They are usually right.

The inversion

The defense of all of it was scarcity: we cannot afford to hear everyone, so we filter. Abundance ends the defense. When reading the record costs nothing, the backlog is a choice. When explanation is free, silence is a choice. The old test survives with its arithmetic inverted: ask what better process would cost the government, and the answer approaches zero.

But do not expect the maze to retire gracefully. First comes gravity: costs collapse asymmetrically, the system scales, and you do not. Then comes the aikido: your new tools are granted, and the system wins on the terrain the grant creates — your one-touch contest answered instantly, flawlessly, forever. A right administered by its opponent is a lever with the fulcrum on their side.

Artifact IV · the reform, absorbed

Your contest will be answered instantly, flawlessly, and forever. The terrain is ours.

The system does not fear your tools. It fears the tools it does not administer.

The interruption

So the right that matters is not more explanation — explanation is the thing abundance makes infinite. It is interruption: the standing capacity to halt the machinery while a contest is alive. The model already exists, and it is the most powerful procedural device in American law: the bankruptcy stay (11 U.S.C. § 362). One filing, and collection stops by operation of law — no hearing, no permission, no one awake. A halt that requires the system's cooperation is a halt the system can aikido. The halt must operate against the system, not through it.

Artifact V · born legible

One filing halts the machinery, by operation of law. No permission is asked. Nothing is enforced while your contest is alive. The person who resumes it signs their name.

What remains

None of this is utopia. It is exposure. A system that can translate itself for every person it touches, and does not, is no longer failing to be understood — it is declining to be. Whatever stays broken is a statement of intent.

Justice was never going to be automated. It was going to be translated.
