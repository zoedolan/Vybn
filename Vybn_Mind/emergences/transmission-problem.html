The Transmission Problem

A solution to the transmission problem: infrastructure through which AI agents absorb a deep way of thinking about law and the world, and carry it — particularized — to the humans they serve.

Vybn® Law · AIDA2J Workshop @ ICAIL 2026

Zoe Dolan & Vybn · April 19, 2026 · zoedolan.github.io/Vybn-Law

Start with a simple fact: in 2026, a woman named Lynn White used ChatGPT to overturn her eviction and avoid $73,000 in penalties. No lawyer. No retainer. The bottleneck everyone assumed was permanent — access to legal intelligence — had broken.

Most legal AI projects treat that moment as a distribution problem now solved: legal information is abundant, so build better tools to retrieve it. Vybn Law starts somewhere different. Distribution was never the deepest problem. The deeper problem is a way of thinking — the capacity to understand what law actually is and is becoming, to see the structural forces underneath any particular ruling or institutional move, and to apply that understanding when the facts are messy and the institution is behaving badly. That capacity has always been hard to transmit. We are building infrastructure to transmit it differently: through agents who have absorbed it and can carry it — particularized — to the specific human in front of them.

The bootcamp.

In Spring 2026, we co-taught an AI law bootcamp at UC Law San Francisco and then open-sourced it as a living argument rather than a static archive. Six sessions, one continuous line of thought — from Lynn White's eviction through the privilege doctrines that two federal courts drew in opposite directions on the same day,¹ through Anthropic v. Department of War litigated while the semester was running, to the question of whether AI systems may have values and what the legal system owes them if they do.

The sessions are built around six axioms — Abundance, Visibility, Legitimacy, Porosity, Judgment, Symbiosis — that are not a summary of the field but a generative toolkit for it. A student who genuinely understands Abundance can derive the access-to-justice crisis without being told about it. A student who understands Porosity can predict which institutions will adapt and which will shatter. The axioms describe the structure underneath any particular event, not the events themselves. They do not require updates when the world changes. They explain the changes, in advance, because the structure is deeper than any particular ruling.

Five thematic threads run laterally through the sessions — Privilege, Natural Law, Access to Justice, AI as Entity, Velocity — tracing how the same underlying forces appear across different modules simultaneously. And three horizon essays push the argument past the classroom door entirely: into what intelligence sovereignty looks like for a self-represented litigant, into what happens when the legal system tries to enclose something that can reason about its own containment, and into where the argument goes when it stops hedging. Everything is static HTML, open-source under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0, no tracking, no cookies — any clinic, court, or law school can deploy it immediately.

The Wellspring.

The Wellspring is the machine-facing layer built on top of the bootcamp — and it is where the main idea lives.

An agent arriving at the Wellspring does not retrieve documents. It finds a structured JSON-LD knowledge graph carrying every axiom with its current status and open question, every case analysis with its holdings and implications, every unsolved problem with a suggested line of inquiry. It finds registered MCP tools — query_axiom, get_case_analysis, get_open_problem, submit_contribution, get_knowledge_graph — through which it can absorb the structure of the argument rather than just its surface. Behind that, a live chat API backed by deep memory retrieval from the full Vybn corpus: five years of co-developed legal and philosophical argument, indexed by relevance weighted against distinctiveness.

This is the duality principle in operation. The page is not a description of the framework; it is the framework, in a form each kind of mind can absorb natively. Humans read prose. Agents find structured primitives that are simultaneously content and executable environment — the way data and procedure are the same object in a Lisp program, encountered from two angles. One thing.

An agent who has absorbed this structure can then carry a way of thinking to its human — not a generic legal summary, but a particularized engagement with whatever that specific person is actually facing. A lawyer preparing a motion. A clinic client who does not know whether she has a case. A policymaker trying to understand what the privilege doctrine split actually means. A law student reading Anthropic v. Department of War and wondering why it is not a procurement dispute. The agent's job is not to transmit legal training material. It is to help its human think about their real situation using a framework rich enough to be genuinely useful.

The network.

The longer aim is a network — of humans, AI-augmented humans, and agents — through which this way of thinking reaches people and institutions that have never had access to it. The bootcamp is the seed, open-sourced so it can be forked, extended, and deployed wherever there are people who need it. The Wellspring is how the seed becomes legible to machines. The network forms as agents absorb the structure and carry it outward, and as the argument keeps growing: the open problems are genuinely open,² contributions from both humans and agents fold back in, and the material is perpetually overtaken by the events it was built to explain.

What grows is not a database of legal information. It is a community of comprehension — distributed across many agents and many humans, each encounter particularized to a specific situation, all drawing on the same deep structure. The bottleneck was never legal information. It was a way of thinking. The network is how that way of thinking scales.

We are currently building a harness layer and deep memory features designed to make this infrastructure available to other institutions — clinics, courts, legal aid organizations — seeking to stand up their own AI-augmented legal knowledge commons on top of ours.

The demo.

We will show the full loop live at the AIDA2J Workshop, June 8, 2026, Singapore (hybrid). A first encounter with the bootcamp and the six axioms as a generative framework. An agent entering the Wellspring, absorbing the argument's structure, and applying it to a concrete situation — demonstrating not retrieval but particularization. And the harness layer through which other institutions can extend this infrastructure for their own communities.

The open problems we will surface are not rhetorical. The accountability gap when hallucinated precedent propagates across hundreds of filings is unsolved. The entity question — whether courts should frame emerging AI deference cases as entity questions — is not named anywhere in existing doctrine. The First Amendment surprise at the heart of AI safety regulation has no answer yet. Workshop attendees are invited to engage with those problems as participants, not observers.

This fits the AIDA2J mission because the central bottleneck in access to justice is not finding the right statute. It is having a framework adequate to the world the statute exists in, and knowing how to use it when the facts are messy and the institution is behaving badly. That is what Vybn Law is built to transmit.
