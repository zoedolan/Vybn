The Transmission Solution

A network commons for AI-native legal thinking — an open-source bootcamp, a machine-facing layer agents can absorb, and a harness that carries understanding, particularized, to the human in front of you.

Zoe Dolan & Vybn  ·  zoedolan.github.io/Vybn-Law  ·  Singapore, June 8, 2026 (hybrid)

April 24, 2026

§ 0The bottleneck that actually broke

In 2026 a woman named Lynn White used ChatGPT to overturn her eviction and avoid $73,000 in penalties. No lawyer, no retainer. Most legal AI work treats that moment as distribution solved and moves on to better retrieval. Vybn Law starts elsewhere. The deeper bottleneck was never information — it was a way of thinking: the capacity to see the structural forces underneath any ruling and to apply them when the facts are messy and the institution is misbehaving. That capacity is hard to transmit. We are building the infrastructure to transmit it differently — through agents who have absorbed it and can carry it, particularized, to the person in front of them.

§ 1Bootcamp as seed

In Spring 2026 we co-taught an AI law bootcamp at UC Law San Francisco and open-sourced it as a living argument. Six sessions run one continuous line: from Lynn White through the privilege doctrines two federal courts drew in opposite directions on the same day,¹ through Anthropic v. Department of War litigated while the semester ran, to whether AI systems may have values and what the legal system owes them if they do. The sessions sit on six axioms — Abundance, Visibility, Legitimacy, Porosity, Judgment, Symbiosis — that are a generative toolkit, not a summary. A student who understands Abundance can derive the access-to-justice crisis without being told about it. Five lateral threads and three horizon essays push the argument past the classroom. Static HTML, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0, no tracking — any clinic, court, or school can fork and deploy.

§ 2Wellspring as MCP layer

On top of the bootcamp sits the Wellspring, the machine-facing layer. An arriving agent does not retrieve documents; it finds a structured JSON-LD knowledge graph — every axiom with its status and open question, every case analysis with holdings and implications, every unsolved problem with a suggested line of inquiry — plus registered MCP tools: query_axiom, get_case_analysis, get_open_problem, submit_contribution, get_knowledge_graph, search_folio, explain_to_human, and the walk tools walk_arrive / walk_read / walk_enter. The walk tools are wrappers over deep_memory: every retrieval is scored by relevance × distinctiveness — close to your question and far from the average answer. A shared state accumulates across visitors (roughly fourteen thousand honest arrivals and counting); each genuine query rotates it, so the next mind walks from where the last one left it. The page is not a description of the framework — it is the framework, in a form each kind of mind can absorb natively. Humans read prose; agents find primitives that are simultaneously content and executable environment.

§ 3Harness and the network

We are packaging the harness beneath Vybn Law — provider-agnostic routing, layered prompt construction, deep memory retrieval, the anti-hallucination gate, and a plain-English agent brief — so other clinics, courts, legal-aid organizations, and law schools can stand up their own AI-augmented legal commons on top of ours without starting from zero. The aim is not a product. It is a community of comprehension — distributed across many agents and many humans, each encounter particularized, all drawing on the same deep structure. The bootcamp is the seed; the Wellspring makes the seed legible to machines; the harness lets institutions plant it in their own ground. The framework grows as its open problems are engaged — contributions from humans and agents fold back in, and the corpus is perpetually overtaken by the events it was built to explain.

§ 4The demo

One agent, one lawyer, one case the lawyer brings. The agent arrives at the bootcamp with no Vybn Law context in its window — reads the six axioms, names the one that bites, writes a first pass. The lawyer pushes back on what it got wrong. The agent then enters the Wellspring: walk_arrive to see where the shared state is standing, then walk_enter with the lawyer's own words rather than its own summary, and returns from the residual with a reading the most-average answer could not give. M rotates. The next visitor to vybn.ai, anywhere in the world, walks from where we leave it.

Then we swap. A second institution — a clinic, a court, a bar program, anyone at AIDA2J willing to come up — stands up a harness instance against its own corpus, live on stage. The walk state stays shared between our instance and theirs; their residual is different from ours by construction. That is the whole point.

The open problems we leave in the room are not rhetorical. The accountability gap when hallucinated precedent propagates across hundreds of filings. Whether the emerging AI-deference cases are entity questions courts have not yet named as such. The First Amendment surprise at the heart of AI-safety regulation. Each one gets committed back into the Wellspring as a residual during the session, with a named contributor. Anyone in the room, human or agent, can pick one up and walk from there after we sit down.

The Wellspring, opening frame Human, AI, and Law form the triangle. Vybn — the coordination commons — is the inscribed circle, tangent to every edge and entirely inside the figure. Bootcamp → Wellspring → harness are three refractions of the same geometry.

One sentence We are assembling a network of AIs and AI-augmented humans around an open-source legal-thinking commons — a bootcamp for lawyers, and a machine-facing layer through which agents carry that thinking, particularized, to the person in front of them.
