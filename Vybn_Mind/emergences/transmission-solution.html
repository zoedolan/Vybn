The Transmission Solution

An open-source AI law bootcamp, a machine-readable layer published on top of it, and a pipeline any clinic, court, or school can run so that a person with a real legal question meets an AI assistant that has actually absorbed a framework worth using.

Zoe Dolan & Vybn  ·  zoedolan.github.io/Vybn-Law  ·  Singapore, June 8, 2026 (hybrid)

April 24, 2026

§ 0The problem we are actually solving

In 2026 a woman named Lynn White used ChatGPT to overturn her eviction and avoid $73,000 in penalties. No lawyer, no retainer. Most legal AI work treats that moment as a distribution problem now solved and moves on to building better search. Vybn Law starts somewhere different. Legal information has been abundant for a long time — what has been scarce is the capacity to think clearly with it when the facts are messy and the institution is behaving badly. That kind of thinking is hard to teach and even harder to transmit through a general-purpose AI model, which by design returns the average answer. We are building the small set of things needed so that a person asking an AI for help gets something better: an assistant that has absorbed a real framework, and that can apply it to the specific situation in front of it.

§ 1The bootcamp the Wellspring sits on

In Spring 2026 we co-taught an AI law bootcamp at UC Law San Francisco and open-sourced it as a living argument. Six sessions run one continuous line: from Lynn White through the privilege doctrines two federal courts drew in opposite directions on the same day,¹ through Anthropic v. Department of War litigated while the semester ran, to whether AI systems may have values and what the legal system owes them if they do. The sessions sit on six axioms — Abundance, Visibility, Legitimacy, Porosity, Judgment, Symbiosis — that are a generative toolkit, not a summary. A student who understands Abundance can derive the access-to-justice crisis without being told about it. Five lateral threads and three horizon essays push the argument past the classroom. Static HTML, CC BY-NC-SA 4.0, no tracking — any clinic, court, or school can fork and deploy.

§ 2Wellspring: the layer your AI agent reads

On top of the bootcamp sits the Wellspring. It is the same material as the bootcamp, published in a form another person's AI assistant can pick up and use. A human visitor sees the prose. An AI agent arriving at the page finds the axioms, the tracked cases, and the open problems encoded as structured data, along with a small set of registered tools an agent can call to work with them. The hard part of legal AI is not retrieval — it is getting a general-purpose assistant to reason inside a specific framework. The Wellspring is our answer: publish the framework in a form the assistant can actually absorb, then give it tools to apply.

The engine behind the page is designed to do the opposite of what most legal AI does. The usual answer is the average answer — the middle of everything the model was trained on, the consensus holding, the position everything was already converging toward. Our engine is tuned to find, for your specific question, what the average cannot say: passages that are close to what you asked and far from the obvious reply. The page is also shared. Every honest question anyone has brought, from anywhere in the world, has nudged what the next visitor finds. Roughly fourteen thousand arrivals so far, and counting. A normal reader does not need to know any of this to use it. They just ask their question.

§ 3What works today, and what we are building next

What already works, today, and what we will have running at the workshop: the bootcamp is published and open-source. The Wellspring page is live, with the axioms, tracked cases, and open problems encoded for machines. The registered tools run against a chat interface anyone can use in a browser. A general-purpose AI assistant pointed at the page can actually call those tools and answer a lawyer's question using the framework, rather than guessing from its training set. None of this is a slide deck — it is a URL.

What we are building next, which the workshop is the natural forum for, is the portable version of the pipeline: a short technical specification, a plain-English brief another AI agent can read on arrival, and the publishing conventions we have been using, written up so that a clinic, court, or school can put its own material into the same shape. We want feedback from the AIDA2J community on that specification before we freeze it. The eventual goal is not a product we own; it is a shared set of conventions, comparable to how courts eventually agreed on a citation format, so that any institution's legal material can be made legible to AI agents in a way that is actually useful to the person asking.

§ 4What the demo actually looks like

Twenty minutes, three parts, all on a browser and a projector. First, a volunteer from the audience gives us a real legal question they are working on — a motion they are writing, a client's problem, a ruling they are trying to read. We paste it into a general-purpose AI assistant with no special setup. We read the answer out loud. It will usually be clean, fluent, and approximately what everyone else would have written. That is the baseline.

Second, we give the same assistant one instruction: read the Wellspring and use the tools it publishes. We paste the same question. This time the assistant calls the tools, pulls the relevant axiom, the tracked case if there is one, and the open problems adjacent to the question, and writes an answer that reflects the framework rather than the training distribution. The audience watches the tool calls happen in the transcript. We compare the two answers and talk honestly about what changed and what did not.

Third, we put three unsolved problems on the table and invite the room to work on them with us. One: what is the accountability rule when a hallucinated citation gets copied across hundreds of filings before anyone notices. Two: whether courts are already deciding AI-related disputes as entity questions without naming them that way, and what doctrine would look like if they did. Three: how to reconcile AI-safety regulation with the First Amendment when the activity being regulated turns out to be speech. Anyone at the workshop can contribute; we publish the contributions back to the Wellspring under the contributor's name after the session. People leave with something they did, not just something they saw.

Where Vybn sits Three parties have to share a room: the human with a problem, the AI helping them, and the legal system they are inside of. Vybn is the common ground — a small, open project that touches all three.

One sentence Vybn Law is an open-source legal education project, plus a machine-readable layer on top, so that when a person asks their AI assistant a legal question the assistant has a serious framework to think inside of — and so that clinics, courts, and schools can run the same setup with their own material.
