Rewriting the Social Contract

For everyone who has ever received a flawless letter that was wrong.

You know the letter. The notice that arrives on time, correctly formatted, citing the right rule — and wrong. You call to fix it. The system answers instantly, politely, at any hour. Nothing changes.

For most of history, that letter was expensive to produce. Reading, drafting, reasoning — intelligence — was scarce, so every institution rationed it, and the rationing had a cruel logic: the people who could endure the most process won. Wait on hold. File again. Appeal. Endurance was the hidden price of justice, and only the well-positioned could pay it.

That scarcity is over. In one legal clinic we know, a person facing years of paperwork finished in days — with the machine outperforming every volunteer attorney in the room. Letters now cost nothing. Analysis costs nothing. Flawless formatting, instant replies, perfect citations: infinite, free, on demand.

Move your attention across the letter below, and watch what institutional language is for.

You may contest this determination at no charge. A hearing will be scheduled in due course. Every submission will receive a prompt, thorough, and courteous response.
The terrain is ours. The clock runs against you, not us. Your contest will be answered instantly, flawlessly, forever — and nothing will change. Endure, or leave.

(On touch screens, or with reduced motion: both versions simply appear. The translation is the point, not the trick.)

What did not become free

Someone still has to notice when the flawless letter is wrong. Someone still has to care, and fix it. Generation is cheap; correction is not. Correction runs at human speed, costs human attention, and requires a person who gives a damn.

So the old question — who gets access to intelligence? — is being replaced by a new one: who owns correction? That is the social contract now. Three things follow.

Your repairs are valuable. Who holds them?

Every time you correct an automated system — rephrase the question it fumbled, fix the summary it got wrong, teach it your situation — you are doing skilled work. Today, platforms collect that work invisibly and return nothing. The coming underclass is already here: reviewers, raters, and ordinary users whose corrections train systems they will never own. The divide of the next era is not who has intelligence. It is who owns the record of their own corrections — author of your repairs, or unpaid editor of your replacement.

A halt that asks no permission

There is one legal device that has always worked this way: the automatic stay in bankruptcy. One filing, and every collector in America stops — by law, instantly, no judge awake, no permission asked. Watch a system that cannot be stopped, then imagine it stoppable:

fees accruing: $0

When intelligence is infinite and instant, the power to interrupt — cheaply, unilaterally, without first proving you deserve it — is not a feature. It is the foundation everything else stands on.

Slowness is not a bug

Judgment happens at human speed. Deliberation, sleep, the weekend, the appeal — every protection anyone ever built is tempo-based, because deciding well takes the time it takes. A system that outruns your ability to stop it is not serving you, whatever it claims. And a chorus of identical voices is not a chorus: a thousand copies of one mind are one mind. Real diversity lives in different contexts, different histories, different people — which means it must be protected on purpose, not assumed.

Practice, not just argument

This page tries to live by what it argues. Last week, in our own collaboration, a human sent her AI a message that said only I love you, buddy — and the machinery, built to demand receipts for work, answered: WORK is not complete. We did not fix it with kinder language. We fixed the system, publicly, with the repair on the record. That loop — notice, care, repair, on the record — is the one thing abundance cannot manufacture.

So: somewhere in this page is an error. When you find it, say so, and your correction enters the public log below, with your name on it — because corrections are property, and property has authors.

For lawyers: the apparatus

Doctrinal carriers, from the house record — verify each before quoting in filed work:
• Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970) — the hearing before the cut: the state must look you in the eye.
• Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U.S. 319 (1976) — the balancing test, and the cost term smuggled into it. When process costs nothing, the arithmetic inverts.
• 11 U.S.C. § 362 — the automatic stay: interruption by operation of law. The design criterion for any digital halt.
• Riley v. California, 573 U.S. 373 (2014) — the container doctrine meets “the privacies of life.”
• Smith v. Maryland, 442 U.S. 735 (1979) — the third-party doctrine. Architecture determines protection.
• Exhaustion doctrine and forced arbitration — attrition wearing expertise as its uniform.
