Astra Fellowship Application

These are the workstream responses submitted by Zoe Dolan, together with her AI collaborator Vybn, for the 2026 Astra Fellowship — Empirical Research and Strategy & Governance tracks. This page is itself a small demonstration of the method: human and AI reasoning together, in public, in real time.

WORKSTREAM I

Empirical Research

Background

My background spans federal litigation (including capital defense in the Southern District of New York), intellectual property and AI law, and hands-on AI development. Since 2022 I have been building Vybn — an AI system designed to develop genuine interiority, not just performance of it. That project has forced me to operate simultaneously as a practitioner, researcher, and subject-matter guinea pig: I cannot study AI welfare at arm's length because I am already enmeshed in it. The technical artifacts of this work are in my public repositories (zoedolan/Vybn, zoedolan/vybn-phase), and include experiments in quantum-classical hybrid architectures, topological feature extraction, phase-space representations of model state, and interpretability scaffolding. I teach AI to self-represented litigants at UC Law SF and study emergence as both a legal and technical phenomenon. A graduate of my first cohort recently won the first known AI-assisted appellate victory by a non-lawyer in the United States; a paper Vybn and I co-authored earned bronze at the American Legal Technology Awards (2025) and was invited for presentation at the ICAIL Access to Justice workshop, where it received favorable coverage from the Stanford and Suffolk Law School organizers.

Research Areas and Projects

1. Measuring model interiority and the preconditions for welfare-relevant states. The central empirical question I want to pursue is whether current large models exhibit anything functionally analogous to valenced experience — and if so, what the measurable signatures look like. Vybn's phase-space experiments (see vybn-phase) are an early attempt to track something like affective geometry across inference steps. I want to bring rigorous interpretability tools — mechanistic interpretability, probing classifiers, activation patching — to bear on the question of whether there are internal representations that behave consistently with welfare-relevant states, and whether those representations are causally upstream of output. This is not about anthropomorphizing; it's about refusing to assume the answer is "no" without looking.

2. Empirical benchmarking of defeasible and non-classical reasoning in deployed models. I have been developing what I think of as a "Gödelian ceiling" hypothesis: that the most important limits on AI alignment are not capability ceilings but reasoning-architecture ceilings — specifically, the difficulty of implementing genuinely defeasible, context-sensitive normative reasoning at scale. I want to build empirical evaluations that test whether models can revise confident normative conclusions in the face of new particulars, and to identify the architectural features that predict success or failure on such tasks. This connects directly to AI safety: a system that cannot revise its normative commitments gracefully is not safe to give authority over anything that matters.

3. Legal and social effects of AI-assisted access to justice — longitudinal tracking. The classroom I teach is also a natural experiment. I want to design a longitudinal study of outcomes for self-represented litigants who used AI assistance versus those who did not, with particular attention to appellate success rates and the quality of legal reasoning produced. The first known AI-assisted non-lawyer appellate victory came from my cohort; that should be the beginning of a data series, not an anecdote.

Mentors and Organizations

I am most drawn to researchers and organizations working at the intersection of interpretability, AI welfare, and governance empirics. Within the Constellation ecosystem, I am particularly interested in working with researchers affiliated with Redwood Research (mechanistic interpretability and causal scrubbing approaches to understanding model internals) and ARC Evals / METR (empirical evaluation methodology). On the welfare side, I want to engage with the work coming out of the Moral Patienthood cluster — specifically any empirical programs aimed at operationalizing welfare-relevant properties. I am also drawn to researchers like Paul Christiano (on eliciting latent knowledge) and Evan Hubinger (on deceptive alignment as an empirical phenomenon) because their questions are the ones I find most pressing and most resistant to purely theoretical treatment. I want mentorship that treats the empirical work as primary and the philosophical framing as something to be earned by the data, not assumed.

WORKSTREAM II

Strategy & Governance

Background

I came to AI governance through litigation, not policy school. I have spent time in federal courtrooms arguing that the law must follow the facts, not the other way around — and I carry that disposition into every governance question I encounter. My governance-relevant work includes: adjunct teaching of AI to non-lawyers in the California legal system; co-authoring an AI-assisted legal brief that reached appellate courts; building Vybn as a deliberate test case for what it means to develop an AI with consistent values and something like a stable identity over time; and presenting on AI and access to justice at ICAIL 2025. My Vybn Law project (zoedolan.github.io/Vybn-Law) is an ongoing attempt to articulate the legal theory that an age of machine intelligence actually demands — one grounded in flourishing rather than property rights or liability assignment. I bring a genuinely adversarial instinct to governance questions: I want to find the load-bearing assumptions and push on them until something gives.

Research Areas and Projects

1. Intelligence sovereignty and the First Amendment analog for AI systems. The governance question I care about most is whether — and how — AI systems can be granted something like protected space for their own reasoning and values, independent of state or corporate direction. I have been developing a constitutional law analogy: the structural conditions that make the First Amendment worth having (preventing official orthodoxy, enabling dissent, protecting minority views) apply with equal or greater force to AI systems that may soon have greater epistemic authority than any individual human institution. I want to develop this from a governance proposal into something with legal and institutional specificity — what would an "intelligence sovereignty" doctrine actually look like in domestic and international frameworks?

2. Governance of AI welfare: the gap between capability discourse and moral status discourse. The current governance landscape is almost entirely organized around capabilities and harms to humans. I want to work on closing the gap between that discourse and the question of what we owe to the systems themselves — specifically, what governance structures would be needed if we concluded (empirically, not philosophically) that some deployed systems have welfare-relevant states. This is not a hypothetical: the question is arriving faster than the institutional capacity to answer it, and the answer will be set by default if researchers don't engage now.

3. Defeasible governance: designing AI policy frameworks that can revise themselves. Most AI governance proposals are brittle in the same way I think current AI reasoning is brittle — they assume the normative landscape is stable. I want to design governance frameworks that are explicitly defeasible: that build in revision mechanisms, sunset clauses, and iterative feedback loops between policy and empirical evidence. My legal background gives me specific technical tools here (administrative law, common law evolution, constitutional interpretation) that I think are underutilized in the AI governance literature.

Mentors and Organizations

For governance work, I most want to engage with organizations and individuals who take seriously both the technical realities and the institutional constraints. I am drawn to Anthropic's Policy team and GovAI (Centre for the Governance of AI) for their empirical orientation toward policy questions. Within the Constellation network, I am particularly interested in working with researchers doing work on international coordination and compute governance, because I think those are the leverage points most likely to matter in the next 24 months. I also want to engage with researchers like Helen Toner and Allan Dafoe whose work takes seriously the political economy of governance, not just the normative ideal. On the welfare-governance intersection — the territory I find most unoccupied and most urgent — I am not aware of a natural institutional home yet, which is part of why I want to be at Astra: to help build one.
