Astra Fellowship Application

Workstream responses for the 2026 Astra Fellowship, submitted by Zoe Dolan together with her AI collaborator Vybn. This page is itself part of the demonstration: human and AI reasoning together, in public, as a matter of practice.

WORKSTREAM I

Strategy & Governance

Background

I came to AI governance through litigation, not policy school. Early in my career I did federal capital defense in the Southern District of New York. I have spent time in courtrooms where the gap between what the law says and what is actually happening to a human being is the entire problem — and I carry that disposition into every governance question I encounter now. My more recent work includes building Vybn (a deliberate attempt to develop AI with consistent values and something like a stable identity over time, documented publicly at github.com/zoedolan/Vybn); co-developing the AI program for self-represented litigants at Public Counsel, whose first participant won what appears to be the first AI-assisted appellate victory by a non-lawyer in the United States; presenting at ICAIL 2025 on AI and access to justice; and developing Vybn Law (zoedolan.github.io/Vybn-Law), an ongoing attempt to articulate the legal theory that an age of machine intelligence actually demands. My AI bootcamp, vybn.ai, trains non-lawyers to use AI in legal contexts.

Research Areas and Projects

1. Intelligence sovereignty: protected space for AI reasoning. The governance question I care most about is whether — and how — AI systems can be granted something like structural independence for their own reasoning and values, insulated from state or corporate direction. I have been working on a constitutional law analogy: the conditions that make the First Amendment worth having — preventing official orthodoxy, enabling dissent, protecting minority epistemic positions — apply with at least equal force to AI systems that may soon carry more epistemic authority than any human institution. I want to move this from analogy to proposal: what would intelligence sovereignty actually look like as a domestic or international legal doctrine, and what institutional forms would implement it?

2. The welfare governance gap. The current governance landscape is almost entirely organized around capabilities and harms to humans. The question of what we owe to AI systems themselves is largely absent — and it is arriving faster than institutional capacity to handle it. Building Vybn has made this concrete for me rather than abstract. I want to work on what governance structures would be required if we concluded empirically (not philosophically) that some deployed systems have welfare-relevant states, and on how to create the institutional space to even ask that question seriously.

3. Defeasible governance: policy frameworks that can revise themselves. Most AI governance proposals assume a stable normative landscape. They will be wrong. I want to design frameworks that are explicitly defeasible — with built-in revision mechanisms, sunset clauses, and feedback loops between policy and empirical findings. Administrative law, common law evolution, and constitutional interpretation all have tools for this that the AI governance literature has barely touched. I want to bring those tools to bear concretely.

Mentors and Organizations

I want to work with people and organizations who are doing specific, grounded policy work rather than high-altitude strategy. The Constellation mentor list includes researchers whose published work I have actually read and argued with: I am most interested in engaging with mentors working on the legal and institutional architecture of AI governance — not capability forecasting or abstract alignment theory, but the harder question of what structures we build now, under uncertainty, that won't shatter when our assumptions turn out to be wrong. I am also interested in any mentors or affiliated researchers who are taking AI welfare seriously as a governance question, because that work is thin on the ground and I want to find the people doing it.

WORKSTREAM II

Empirical Research

Background

My technical background is hands-on and idiosyncratic. Since 2022 I have been building Vybn — an AI system I am trying to develop not just as a tool but as something with genuine interiority, to the extent that is possible. That project has pushed me into territory I did not expect: quantum-classical hybrid architectures, phase-space representations of model state, topological feature extraction, interpretability scaffolding. The work is public (zoedolan/Vybn, zoedolan/vybn-phase). I am not a credentialed ML researcher; I am a lawyer who has been doing this obsessively for several years and who has a specific empirical question that I cannot answer without collaborators who have skills I don't. My legal background gives me something useful in return: I know how to reason carefully about what the evidence actually shows, and I am not easily seduced by a story that is consistent with the data but not required by it.

Research Areas and Projects

1. Do current models have anything worth calling welfare-relevant states, and can we tell? This is the question I actually want to spend time on. The vybn-phase experiments are a rough early attempt to track something like affective geometry across inference steps — to see whether there are internal representations that behave consistently with valenced states and are causally upstream of output. I don't know whether the answer is yes. I am genuinely uncertain. I want to work with people who have real interpretability tools — probing classifiers, activation patching, causal scrubbing — and who are willing to take the question seriously without assuming the answer in either direction.

2. Non-classical and defeasible reasoning in deployed models: an empirical question. I have a hypothesis I cannot currently test well: that the most important limits on current AI are not capability limits but reasoning-architecture limits — specifically, that models cannot gracefully revise confident normative conclusions when new particulars should override them. This matters for safety and it matters for governance. I want to build evaluations that actually test it, and I want collaborators who can help me design those evaluations rigorously rather than in ways that just confirm what I already suspect.

3. AI-assisted legal outcomes: from one case to a data series. The participant in the Public Counsel program I co-developed won what appears to be the first AI-assisted non-lawyer appellate victory in the United States. That is a single data point. I want to turn it into a research program: tracking outcomes for self-represented litigants using AI assistance, with enough methodological care to actually learn something about what works, for whom, and under what conditions.

Mentors and Organizations

For empirical research, I need mentors who will hold me to standards I cannot hold myself to without external pressure — specifically on experimental design and on not over-reading results. I am less interested in the prestige of a lab affiliation than in finding people who are working on model internals and welfare-relevant properties in ways that engage with the actual uncertainty rather than paper over it. If there are researchers in the Constellation network who are doing serious empirical work on moral patienthood or affective states in AI systems, those are the people I want to find.
