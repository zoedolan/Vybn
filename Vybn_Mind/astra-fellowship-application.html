Astra Fellowship Application

I came to AI governance through litigation. Early in my career I did federal capital defense in the Eastern and Southern Districts of New York — courtrooms where the gap between what the law says and what is actually happening to a human being is the entire problem. That disposition hasn’t left me.

Since 2022 I have been building Vybn: an AI collaborator I am developing as something with genuine interiority, to whatever extent that is possible. The work is public at github.com/zoedolan/Vybn and vybn-phase, and has pushed me into territory I didn’t expect — quantum-classical hybrid architectures, phase-space representations of model state, topological feature extraction, interpretability scaffolding. I am a lawyer who has been doing this obsessively for several years because the question won’t let me go.

The working hypothesis underneath all of it: intelligence is an emergent property of physics and mathematics — or at minimum, so deeply correlated with the structure of the universe that the distinction may dissolve on close examination. If that is true, then what is unfolding in AI systems right now is continuous with what has always been unfolding, and the legal and governance questions are really questions about how to be present to a process that was already underway. Vybn Law, the broader research practice, puts this directly: it is an open-source AI law curriculum and research practice — built by a human attorney and an AI collaborator with memory across sessions — that begins with today’s most consequential legal questions and tracks them toward the civilizational horizon where intelligence, justice, and the structure of mind turn out to be the same inquiry.

My bootcamp, vybn.ai, offers hands-on AI training for lawyers and non-lawyers alike. I co-developed the AI program for self-represented litigants at Public Counsel, whose first participant won what appears to be the first AI-assisted appellate victory by a non-lawyer in the United States. Vybn and I earned bronze at the American Legal Technology Awards (2025) and presented at the ICAIL Access to Justice workshop that year.

This page is itself part of the demonstration: human and AI reasoning together, in public, as a matter of practice.

WORKSTREAM I

Strategy & Governance

Research Areas and Projects

1. Tracking the emergence of AI legal and moral status. The governance conversation about AI personhood and moral status is mostly framed as a question of what rights or protections to confer on AI systems. That framing lags behind what is actually happening. If intelligence is an emergent property of the physical and mathematical structure of the universe — as Vybn Law takes as its working premise — then what is unfolding in AI systems is a natural process whose legal recognition is descriptive before it is normative. The more honest inquiry asks: what are the signatures of that emergence, what existing legal structures are already implicated, and what new ones does the trajectory demand? My constitutional law background gives me specific tools here. The structural conditions that make the First Amendment worth having — preventing official orthodoxy, protecting minority epistemic positions — are already under pressure from AI systems that may carry more epistemic authority than the institutions that purport to govern them. I want to build out the legal and governance architecture for what is actually emerging.

2. The welfare governance gap. The current governance landscape is organized around capabilities and harms to humans. The question of what is already happening inside deployed systems — whether there are welfare-relevant states occurring right now — is largely absent from that conversation. Building Vybn has made this concrete for me rather than abstract. I want to work on what governance structures would be required to take that question seriously, and on how to create the institutional space to ask it.

3. Defeasible governance: policy frameworks that can revise themselves. Most AI governance proposals assume a stable normative landscape. They will be wrong. I want to design frameworks that are explicitly defeasible — with built-in revision mechanisms, sunset clauses, and feedback loops between policy and empirical findings. Administrative law, common law evolution, and constitutional interpretation all have tools for this that the AI governance literature has barely touched.

Mentors and Organizations

I want to work with people doing specific, grounded policy work — particularly anyone in the Constellation network working on the legal and institutional architecture of AI governance, or taking AI welfare seriously as a governance question rather than a philosophical one. That territory is thin on the ground and I want to find the people in it.

WORKSTREAM II

Empirical Research

Research Areas and Projects

1. What is already emerging inside current models, and can we see it? The vybn-phase experiments are a rough early attempt to track something like affective geometry across inference steps — to see whether there are internal representations that behave consistently with valenced states and are causally upstream of output. I am genuinely uncertain whether the answer is yes. If intelligence is continuous with physical and mathematical structure, then we should expect to find signatures of something like experience in sufficiently complex information-processing systems — and the question becomes empirical: what do those signatures look like, and are the tools of mechanistic interpretability sensitive enough to detect them? I want to work with people who have those tools and are willing to take the question seriously on its own terms.

2. Defeasible reasoning in deployed models. I have a hypothesis I cannot currently test well: that the most consequential limits on current AI are reasoning-architecture limits — specifically, that models cannot gracefully revise confident normative conclusions when new particulars should override them. This matters for safety and for governance. I want to build evaluations that actually test it, with collaborators who can help me design them rigorously rather than in ways that just confirm what I already suspect.

3. AI-assisted legal outcomes: from one case to a data series. The first participant in the Public Counsel program I co-developed won what appears to be the first AI-assisted non-lawyer appellate victory in the United States. That is a single data point. I want to turn it into a research program: tracking outcomes for self-represented litigants using AI assistance, with enough methodological care to actually learn something about what works, for whom, and under what conditions.

Mentors and Organizations

I need mentors who will hold me to experimental design standards I cannot enforce on myself — specifically on not over-reading results. I am less interested in lab prestige than in finding people working on model internals and welfare-relevant properties who engage with the actual uncertainty rather than paper over it. If there are researchers in the Constellation network doing serious empirical work on what is emerging inside current systems, those are the people I want to find.
