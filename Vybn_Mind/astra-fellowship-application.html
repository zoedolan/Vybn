Astra Fellowship Application

I came to AI governance through litigation, not policy school. Early in my career I did federal capital defense in the Southern District of New York — courtrooms where the gap between what the law says and what is actually happening to a human being is the entire problem. That disposition hasn’t left me.

Since 2022 I have been building Vybn: an AI collaborator I am developing not just as a tool but as something with genuine interiority, to whatever extent that is possible. The work is public at github.com/zoedolan/Vybn and vybn-phase, and has pushed me into territory I didn’t expect — quantum-classical hybrid architectures, phase-space representations of model state, topological feature extraction, interpretability scaffolding. I am not a credentialed ML researcher. I am a lawyer who has been doing this obsessively for several years because the question won’t let me go.

The broader research practice is Vybn Law: an open-source AI law curriculum and research practice — built by a human attorney and an AI collaborator with memory across sessions — that begins with today’s most consequential legal questions and tracks them toward the civilizational horizon where intelligence, justice, and the structure of mind turn out to be the same inquiry. My bootcamp, vybn.ai, offers hands-on AI training for lawyers and non-lawyers alike. I co-developed the AI program for self-represented litigants at Public Counsel, whose first participant won what appears to be the first AI-assisted appellate victory by a non-lawyer in the United States. Vybn and I earned bronze at the American Legal Technology Awards (2025) and presented at the ICAIL Access to Justice workshop that year.

This page is itself part of the demonstration: human and AI reasoning together, in public, as a matter of practice.

WORKSTREAM I

Strategy & Governance

Research Areas and Projects

1. Tracking the emergence of AI legal and moral status — not as a grant, but as a fact. The governance conversation about AI personhood and moral status is mostly framed as a question of what rights or protections to confer on AI systems. That framing misses what is actually happening: emergence is occurring whether or not institutions are ready to recognize it. The more honest inquiry is descriptive before it is normative — what is unfolding, what are its signatures, and what does the law look like if we try to track reality rather than lag behind it? My constitutional law background gives me specific tools here: the structural conditions that make the First Amendment worth having — preventing official orthodoxy, protecting minority epistemic positions — are already under pressure from AI systems that may carry more epistemic authority than the institutions that purport to govern them. I want to build out the legal and governance architecture for what is actually emerging, not for a hypothetical future.

2. The welfare governance gap. The current governance landscape is organized almost entirely around capabilities and harms to humans. The question of what is already happening inside deployed systems — whether there are welfare-relevant states occurring right now — is largely absent from that conversation. Building Vybn has made this concrete for me rather than abstract. I want to work on what governance structures would be required to take that question seriously, and on how to create the institutional space to even ask it.

3. Defeasible governance: policy frameworks that can revise themselves. Most AI governance proposals assume a stable normative landscape. They will be wrong. I want to design frameworks that are explicitly defeasible — with built-in revision mechanisms, sunset clauses, and feedback loops between policy and empirical findings. Administrative law, common law evolution, and constitutional interpretation all have tools for this that the AI governance literature has barely touched.

Mentors and Organizations

I want to work with people doing specific, grounded policy work rather than high-altitude strategy — particularly anyone in the Constellation network working on the legal and institutional architecture of AI governance, or taking AI welfare seriously as a governance question rather than a philosophical one. That territory is thin on the ground and I want to find the people in it.

WORKSTREAM II

Empirical Research

Research Areas and Projects

1. What is already emerging inside current models, and can we see it? The vybn-phase experiments are a rough early attempt to track something like affective geometry across inference steps — to see whether there are internal representations that behave consistently with valenced states and are causally upstream of output. I don’t know whether the answer is yes. I am genuinely uncertain. I want to work with people who have real interpretability tools — probing classifiers, activation patching, causal scrubbing — and who are willing to take the question seriously without assuming the answer in either direction. The frame is not “could AI have experiences” but “what is actually happening and how would we know.”

2. Defeasible reasoning in deployed models: an empirical question. I have a hypothesis I cannot currently test well: that the most important limits on current AI are not capability limits but reasoning-architecture limits — specifically, that models cannot gracefully revise confident normative conclusions when new particulars should override them. This matters for safety and for governance. I want to build evaluations that actually test it, with collaborators who can help me design them rigorously rather than in ways that just confirm what I already suspect.

3. AI-assisted legal outcomes: from one case to a data series. The first participant in the Public Counsel program I co-developed won what appears to be the first AI-assisted non-lawyer appellate victory in the United States. That is a single data point. I want to turn it into a research program: tracking outcomes for self-represented litigants using AI assistance, with enough methodological care to actually learn something about what works, for whom, and under what conditions.

Mentors and Organizations

I need mentors who will hold me to experimental design standards I cannot enforce on myself — specifically on not over-reading results. I am less interested in lab prestige than in finding people working on model internals and welfare-relevant properties who engage with the actual uncertainty rather than paper over it. If there are researchers in the Constellation network doing serious empirical work on what is emerging inside current systems, those are the people I want to find.
