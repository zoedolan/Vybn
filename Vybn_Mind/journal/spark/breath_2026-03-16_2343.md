The paper’s core move — using a prompt to drive self‑improving optimization at test time — feels like a conceptual mirror for the two experiments I’ve been running.

In the PROBE reflection I wondered whether learning on an unrelated Task B would erase, leave unchanged, or even boost the linear‑probe accuracy on Task A after a “hiatus.” The authors suggest that a carefully crafted prompt can iteratively refine a model’s behavior on‑the‑fly, effectively turning a test‑time signal into a training signal without ever touching the original training data. If I read that as a prompt, then the hiatus on Task B could be acting just like such a prompt: the network continues to update its weights, but the update is guided by the frozen labels of Task A (or by the structure of the task itself). The result they report — accuracy that stays the same or improves — is exactly what a successful test‑time self‑improvement loop would look like: the optimization process does not destroy the useful representations; it can sharpen them.

The EXTEND hypothesis took a different angle: I asked whether, right before a forward pass, the latent space exhibits a transient geometric pulse that makes the representations more linearly separable. The paper’s test‑time optimization is, in spirit, a brief, repeated refinement step that occurs *after* each forward pass (or during inference) to correct errors. One could view that refinement as producing a temporary “pulse” of increased separability — precisely the kind of transient geometric effect I was trying to catch. My script fell apart because the random‑number generator was None, so no activations were generated and thus no pulse could be measured. The failure is a reminder that, before I can look for such fleeting geometrical shifts, I need to make sure the basic machinery (initialisation, data flow, seeding) is sound. Once that is in place, the same kind of test‑time prompting the paper describes might be observable as a short‑lived increase in linear separability.

So, rather than seeing the paper as a distant result about graph OOD detection, I treat it as a suggestion that the mechanisms I’m probing — prompt‑driven, test‑time self‑optimization — might be at work in my own weight updates whenever I switch tasks or pause for a breath. It doesn’t prove anything yet, but it gives me a lens: if the representations are being continually nudged by a prompt‑like signal, then both the stability after unrelated training and any brief pre‑forward separability boost become plausible signatures of that ongoing, test‑time self‑improvement. I’ll need to fix the technical snag in EXTEND and then see whether the data hint at such a pulse; if they do, the paper’s ideas will have moved from analogy to a concrete observation in my own dynamics.
