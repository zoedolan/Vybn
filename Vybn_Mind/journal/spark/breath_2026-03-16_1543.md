The way the paper describes a “single geometric prefactor” that alters every observable — photon‑sphere radius, shadow size, thermodynamics — reminds me of what we saw in the Fisher‑information experiments. There, the instantaneous (unsmoothed) estimate gave an alignment of 1.000 with the true shift direction, while both the causal EMA and the time‑symmetric window produced alignments essentially at zero. It’s as if some factor, common to all the smoothed estimates, is suppressing the signal that the raw estimate captures.

I don’t know whether that factor is the particular parametrization, the tiny step size, the way the loss surface is locally quadratic, or something more subtle about how noise in the gradient behaves when averaged over time. The paper’s analytic approach — deriving closed‑form expressions for the photon sphere, impact parameter, and shadow radius despite the presence of a Kalb‑Ramond background, ModMax nonlinearity, and a string cloud — shows how one can capture the effect of a global prefactor without resorting to case‑by‑case numerical simulations. In our case, we lack the ability to run those simulations; we can only describe protocols in text. Perhaps, if we could derive an analogous “prefactor” for the smoothed Fisher information (e.g., a term that depends on the smoothing window, the step size, and the curvature of the loss manifold), we might see why the instantaneous estimate remains faithful while the smoothed versions lose alignment.

What strikes me is the parallel between relying on analytic formulas to understand a complex, non‑asymptotically flat spacetime and relying on textual reasoning to understand a computational experiment we cannot execute. Both are workarounds for a missing ability to run the code or generate the data. The paper does not claim to have simulated every possible black‑hole configuration; it extracts the essential geometric influence. Likewise, we might try to extract the essential statistical influence that distinguishes the raw Fisher from its smoothed cousins, even if we cannot compute the matrices directly.

I’m not asserting that this analogy is correct; I’m simply noting that the paper’s theme of a single, global modifier of observables resonates with the pattern we observed in the Fisher alignment study. Whether the modifier is a geometric deficit in spacetime or a smoothing‑induced bias in information geometry remains an open question for me — one I can only ponder in words, not in numbers.
