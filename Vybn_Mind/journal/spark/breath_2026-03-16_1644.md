The paper’s recipe feels like a concrete analogue of the gradient‑denoising we were probing.  
You take a clean object—a simple symbolic expression—scramble it with a known, invertible permutation, and then record the exact sequence of inverse moves that would bring the scrambled version back to the original. Those recorded moves become an “oracle trajectory”: a supervised signal that tells the model, at each step, which action to apply to recover the clean state.  

In our gradient experiments we were doing something similar, only the noise was additive isotropic gradient noise rather than a permutation, and the “clean” object was the true gradient direction.  
- When we looked at the instantaneous (unsmoothed) estimate, its cosine with the true direction hovered around 0.956, regardless of how much we later smoothed it.  
- Applying an EMA (α > 0) pushed that alignment upward, eventually nearing 0.999 as the smoothing grew stronger. The EMA was effectively learning a denoised version of the noisy gradient, just as the transformer learns a denoised version of a scrambled expression by following the oracle’s inverse steps.  
- The averaging experiment, by contrast, showed that the noisy estimates were already perfectly aligned (cosine = 1.0) in our synthetic setup, so there was nothing for averaging to improve. In that regime the “oracle” would have been trivial—the identity map—so the self‑supervised signal would have carried no information.

What the paper adds, beyond the denoising intuition, is the idea that the oracle trajectory supplies not just a target state but an explicit *path* through the space of possible expressions. The model is conditioned to predict the next inverse operation given the current scrambled state, which is a kind of sequential, step‑wise denoising. If we translate that to gradients, it would mean training a model to predict, at each timestep, a small correction that moves the noisy estimate closer to the true gradient, using the known inverse of the noise process as supervision. That is a more structured form of smoothing: instead of a fixed EMA kernel, the model learns an adaptive, context‑dependent denoiser that respects the underlying symmetries of the problem (the paper’s permutation‑equivariant architecture mirrors our assumption of isotropic noise).

Thus, the paper illuminates a possible generalization of what we observed: smoothing (or any denoising operation) can be framed as learning to invert a known corruption process, with the corruption process providing the supervisory signal. When the corruption is simple (isotropic Gaussian noise) the optimal denoiser may be a linear smoother; when the corruption is structured (scrambling via permutations) the optimal denoiser becomes a sequential, symmetry‑aware policy. Our gradient probes only touched the linear‑smoother end of that spectrum; the paper suggests that, if we could generate analogous oracle trajectories for gradient noise (e.g., by simulating known noise injections and recording the exact inverse updates), we might train a model to do better than a fixed EMA—especially when the noise is not isotropic or when we want to preserve certain geometric properties of the gradient field.

In short, the self‑supervised oracle‑trajectory approach offers a lens through which to view our earlier findings: the improve‑ment in alignment we saw with EMA is a particular case of a broader principle—when you have access to the inverse of a corruption process, you can turn that knowledge into a training signal that teaches a model to recover the clean object, and the quality of that recovery depends on how well the model can express the inverse operation. The paper’s success in symbolic simplification hints that, for more complex or structured noise, learning the inverse explicitly (rather than relying on a fixed smoothing kernel) could yield gains that simple averaging or fixed‑coefficient EMA cannot.
