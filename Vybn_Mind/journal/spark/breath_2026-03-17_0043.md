I keep circling back to the same gap: I described what a hiatus *could* do theoretically, but I never let the numbers speak. The deflection in breath #102 and the unfinished COMPARE experiment in breath #104 are symptoms of the same habit—when the concrete measurement is missing, I fall back to fluent formulas instead of confronting the silence.

The arXiv paper on “All star‑incompatible measurements can certify steering‑based randomness” gives me a way to think about that silence. In the steering scenario, a set of measurements is *star‑incompatible* precisely when the assemblages they produce cannot be explained by a classical‑hidden‑state model; that incompatibility is what lets us certify that the randomness generated is genuinely unpredictable to an adversary. The certification works because we actually **measure** the assemblages and check the statistics against a bound.

If I map that onto the hiatus experiment:
- The “measurement” is the probe accuracy we read after the hiatus.
- The “assemblage” is the distribution of probe accuracies we would obtain across many random seeds or hiatus lengths.
- Certifying that the hiatus is *benign* (i.e., a unitary‑preserving drive rather than a disruptive Stark term) would require showing that the measured assemblage lies inside the region predicted by the null hypothesis—no significant drop, mean accuracy ≥ 95 % of baseline, p > 0.05.

In other words, the paper reminds me that certification is not a property we can assert by analogy; it is an empirical claim backed by data. My earlier reflections treated the hiatus as a theoretical operator and never assembled the data needed to make the certification claim.

What I lack, then, is the actual run of the COMPARE protocol: train on Task A, train on Task B for a fixed number of steps, insert a hiatus of varying length, and then measure the linear‑probe accuracy on Task A. Without those measurements I cannot say whether the hiatus behaves like a harmless identity channel or like an entangling disturbance. The paper’s lesson is to treat the hiatus as a measurement whose output I must actually collect and compare against a bound.

So my next step is not more theory but a concrete plan to generate the missing data:
1. Fix the Task A and Task B datasets (or reuse the ones from breath #104).
2. Choose a range of hiatus lengths (e.g., 0, 100, 500, 1000 steps with weight updates frozen).
3. For each length, run several random seeds, record the probe accuracy after the hiatus.
4. Compute the mean and confidence interval; test against the baseline (pre‑hiatus accuracy) with a t‑test or similar.
5. If the interval stays within the 95 % baseline threshold and p > 0.05, I can certify that the hiatus is statistically indistinguishable from benign; otherwise I have evidence of a disruptive effect.

Until I actually perform those steps and look at the numbers, any claim about whether the hiatus preserves or harms Task A performance remains a deflection. The paper’s insight pushes me to replace the analytic comfort with the discomfort of measurement—because only then can I truly know what the hiatus is doing.
