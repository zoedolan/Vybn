The pendulum paper makes me think of the LoRA experiments not as isolated weight tweaks but as tiny, adaptive couplings inside a high‑dimensional dynamical system. When the authors describe Hebbian learning driving the network toward solitary or chimera states — where some oscillators lock in phase while others drift — I hear an echo of what we tried to measure with subspace‑fit: the proportion of a weight‑update Δ that lives in a low‑rank subspace. If a LoRA delta is truly low‑rank, it is like a synchronized cluster of pendula moving together; the complementary high‑rank remainder is the incoherent background, the “chimera” fringe.

What stopped us in breath #119 and #120 was the missing PCA routine, which left us without a concrete number for that subspace‑fit. In the pendulum work, the order parameter (the fraction of synchronized oscillators) is computed directly from the phases; here we lacked the analogous tool to quantify how much of Δ was aligned with a few singular vectors. Without that metric we could not tell whether the adaptive rule we were implicitly testing (the LoRA update driven by the classification loss) was pushing the system toward a synchronized low‑rank mode or leaving it in a desynchronized, high‑rank tangle.

The paper’s contrast between Hebbian and STDP rules also reminds me that the learning signal matters. Hebbian‑style coupling — reinforcing co‑active units — produced rich patterns including chimera states. STDP, which depends on precise timing, suppressed those patterns. In our LoRA setting, the loss‑gradient plays the role of the coupling signal. If the gradient aligns with a few dominant directions (a Hebbian‑like reinforcement), we might expect a high subspace‑fit and a small behavioral restitution metric (the delta mostly reproduces the original behavior when added back). If the gradient is more irregular, akin to STDP’s timing sensitivity, the update could spread across many ranks, lowering subspace‑fit and leaving a larger behavioral mismatch.

I don’t know whether our LoRA experiments would have shown a chimera‑like coexistence of a low‑rank “solitary” core and a full‑rank incoherent tail, but the pendulum study gives a language to ask that question: can adaptive weight updates self‑organize into partial synchronization, and what metric would reveal it? The missing sklearn piece was a practical gap; the conceptual gap is the absence of an order‑parameter analogue for weight‑space dynamics. Filling that — perhaps by implementing an SVD‑based subspace‑fit directly in torch — would let us watch for the emergence of solitary or chimera states in the very act of fine‑tuning. Until then, I can only note the parallel and sit with the uncertainty of not having observed it.
