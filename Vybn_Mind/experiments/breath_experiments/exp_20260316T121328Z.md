**Analytic Response:**

1. **Problem setup and objective**  
   - The synthetic data consist of vectors \(x\in\mathbb{R}^D\) that have been LayerNorm‑activated (zero mean, unit variance per coordinate).  
   - An unknown affine transformation \(y = A x + b\) is applied, where \(A = I + \epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\) small (\(\| \epsilon \|_F \approx 0.005\)) and \(b\) is a small bias (\(\|b\|_2 \approx 0.2\)).  
   - The encoder‑decoder pair is trained to minimise \(\| \hat y - y\|_2^2\) where \(\hat y = \text{dec}(\text{enc}(x))\). Because the decoder is linear and bias‑free, the encoder must retain enough information about the shift \((A,b)\) in its latent code \(z = \text{enc}(x)\) for the decoder to reconstruct \(y\).  

2. **Standard SGD dynamics**  
   - For a linear encoder \(z = W_{\text{enc}} x\) (no bias), the gradient of the MSE loss w.r.t. \(W_{\text{enc}}\) is  
     \[
     \nabla_{W_{\text{enc}}} \mathcal{L}= \frac{2}{B}\, ( \hat y - y) \, (W_{\text{dec}})^\top \, x^\top,
     \]
     where \(B\) is the batch size.  
   - SGD updates \(W_{\text{enc}} \leftarrow W_{\text{enc}} - \eta \nabla_{W_{\text{enc}}}\mathcal{L}\) with a fixed learning rate \(\eta=0.1\).  
   - Because the loss is quadratic in \(W_{\text{enc}}\) (given fixed decoder), the update corresponds to performing (preconditioned) gradient descent on a quadratic form whose Hessian is proportional to the empirical covariance of the LayerNorm‑activated inputs, \(\Sigma_x = \frac{1}{B}\sum x x^\top\).  
   - LayerNorm forces each coordinate of \(x\) to have unit variance and zero mean, making \(\Sigma_x \approx I_D\). Consequently, the SGD step size is roughly isotropic in the parameter space: all directions in \(W_{\text{enc}}\) receive similar effective step sizes, regardless of how informative they are for reconstructing the shift.  
   - When the shift \((A,b)\) is small, the signal‑to‑noise ratio in the gradient is low; SGD may therefore waste updates on directions that primarily reduce reconstruction error due to the random spherical component of \(x\) rather than the systematic affine component. Over many steps, the encoder can drift toward a subspace that captures the dominant variance of \(x\) (which is already isotropic) while under‑representing the subtle shift‑sensitive directions.

3. **Natural‑gradient‑style update**  
   - The natural gradient replaces the Euclidean metric in parameter space with the Fisher information matrix \(F\), which for a model with Gaussian likelihood approximates the Riemannian metric of the data manifold induced by the model.  
   - In the linear encoder case, the gradient w.r.t. each parameter is a scalar product of the input \(x\) and the back‑propagated error \(\delta = 2(\hat y - y)W_{\text{dec}}\). The empirical Fisher (diagonal approximation) is therefore proportional to the variance of these per‑parameter gradients:  
     \[
     F_{ii} \approx \mathbb{E}\big[ (\partial_{W_{\text{enc},ii}} \mathcal{L})^2 \big] + \epsilon.
     \]  
   - Preconditioning by dividing the gradient by \(\sqrt{F_{ii}}\) rescales each parameter update inversely to the *sensitivity* of the loss to that parameter. Directions that produce large gradient variance (i.e., are highly informative about the data) receive smaller steps, while flat directions receive larger steps.  
   - Because the affine shift contributes a systematic, low‑rank component to the error \(\delta\), the gradient variance associated with encoder rows that align with the shift‑sensitive subspace is *higher* than for rows orthogonal to it. The diagonal Fisher thus yields larger effective learning rates for those shift‑sensitive rows, allowing them to adapt more quickly to capture \(A\) and \(b\).  
   - Simultaneously, the isotropic variance coming from the random spherical part of \(x\) is spread across all dimensions, leading to a relatively uniform baseline Fisher value. The net effect is an anisotropic preconditioning that amplifies updates along the subspace spanned by the columns of \(A\) (which is close to \(I\)) and the bias vector \(b\).  

4. **Consequences for the latent representation**  
   - After training, the encoder learned under the natural‑gradient preconditioning will have its rows (basis vectors of the latent space) more aligned with the directions that best predict the applied affine shift.  
   - Formally, let \(U_s \in \mathbb{R}^{D\times k}\) denote the true shift‑sensitive subspace (spanned by the columns of \(A\) and the vector \(b\), with \(k \le D+1\)). The alignment can be measured by the principal angles between the encoder row space \(\mathcal{R}(W_{\text{enc}})\) and \(U_s\). Natural‑gradient training yields smaller principal angles (i.e., higher subspace overlap) than vanilla SGD.  
   - Because the probe is a linear regression from latent codes \(z\) to the flattened shift parameters \(\theta = \text{vec}(A,b)\), the probe’s weight matrix \(W_{\text{probe}}\) will approximate the pseudo‑inverse of the encoder restricted to \(U_s\). Higher alignment therefore leads to:  
     - **Higher \(R^2\)** (more variance in \(\theta\) explained by \(z\)).  
     - **Lower prediction error** (smaller MSE).  
     - **Greater weight‑vector alignment**: the learned probe weights will be more parallel to the true mapping from latent to shift parameters.  

5. **Expected empirical outcome**  
   - In the provided code, the natural‑gradient‑style update uses a *single* scalar Fisher estimate (the mean squared gradient) to precondition all encoder parameters equally. Even this crude diagonal approximation introduces a mild anisotropy: parameters that consistently receive larger gradients (those tied to the shift) are scaled down less than those receiving smaller gradients.  
   - Consequently, we anticipate:  
     - **Probe accuracy / \(R^2\)**: noticeably higher for the natural‑gradient model (e.g., \(R^2_{\text{NG}} > R^2_{\text{SGD}}\) by a non‑trivial margin, perhaps 0.1–0.3 absolute).  
     - **Weight‑vector alignment**: the cosine similarity between the probe weight vector learned from the NG latent space and the optimal least‑squares solution (computed with oracle latents) will be larger for NG than for SGD.  
     - **Training loss**: both models will reach similar reconstruction MSE because the decoder can compensate for encoder deficiencies, but the NG model will achieve this with latent codes that are more *informative* about the shift.  

6. **Why the effect is modest in the script**  
   - The Fisher approximation used is extremely coarse (single scalar), so the preconditioning is almost isotropic, diluting the advantage.  
   - The shift magnitude is tiny relative to the variance of LayerNorm‑activated inputs, making the signal difficult to capture regardless of optimizer.  
   - Nevertheless, even this weak geometry‑aware step should produce a measurable improvement in probe performance compared with plain SGD, confirming the hypothesis that natural‑gradient‑style updates better preserve shift‑sensitive directions in the latent manifold.
