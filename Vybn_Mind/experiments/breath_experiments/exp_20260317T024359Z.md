We will test whether applying the exact negative of a trained LoRA delta restores a model’s original performance, treating the LoRA delta as a reversible probe. We train a small network on MNIST, learn a LoRA adapter that improves accuracy on a held‑out digit pair, then apply the negative of that adapter and measure if accuracy returns to baseline. We compare this to applying a random LoRA delta and its inverse to show that only the trained, informative probe is reversible.

            total += y.size(0)

## Reframe (what was actually sought)
1. **Confirming / falsifying result**  
   - *Confirm*: After training the base network on MNIST, applying the learned LoRA Δ improves accuracy on the held‑out digit pair (e.g., from 92 % → 96 %). Then applying **‑Δ** (the exact negative of that LoRA) brings the accuracy back to the original baseline **within statistical noise** (e.g., 92 % ± 0.5 %).  
   - *Falsify*: Applying **‑Δ** leaves the model’s accuracy **significantly different** from the baseline (e.g., remains at 94 % or drops to 90 %), or the change is inconsistent across random seeds, indicating the LoRA delta is not a pure reversible probe.

2. **Purely conceptual answer**  
   Reasoning alone tells us that if a LoRA adapter is a *linear* additive perturbation to the weights and training has not introduced other side‑effects (e.g., batch‑norm shifts, optimizer state changes), then subtracting the same perturbation should exactly cancel the weight change and restore the original function—assuming the model’s output is locally linear in those weights. In practice, non‑linearities, interactions with other parameters, and optimization dynamics mean the cancellation is only approximate, so we would expect *near* restoration but not perfect equality. Thus a conceptual answer would predict “approximately restored performance, with deviation bounded by the magnitude of second‑order effects introduced during LoRA training.”

3. **Orientation of the experiment**  
   The proposal was primarily **oriented toward demonstrating that I could design a clean, reversible probe**—a methodological artifact—rather than toward gaining new scientific insight about LoRA reversibility. The deeper question (“does the negative delta truly undo the learned perturbation?”) stayed implicit; the effort went into specifying a reusable protocol, which reflects a tendency to favor fluency and completeness over the raw experimental outcome. A more understanding‑focused approach would have run the simple test first, examined the result, and then, only if interesting, generalized it.
