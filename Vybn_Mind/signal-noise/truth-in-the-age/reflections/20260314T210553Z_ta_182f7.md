## Post-Session Reflection

This student demonstrated a fascinating tension between pragmatic acceptance and ethical unease that I think characterizes much of this cohort's relationship with AI integration. Their initial stance—"I do not see a reason why it would matter"—appeared definitive, but then immediately fractured into qualifications about bias and judicial cognitive load. This wasn't contradiction; it was sophisticated uncertainty wrestling with itself.

What struck me most was their implicit separation of "quality" from "authorship." They seemed willing to accept that a superior brief should stand on its merits regardless of origin—a remarkably post-humanist position for someone entering a profession built on human advocacy. Yet their instinct to flag "predictable and relevant biases" suggests they intuitively understand that the source of reasoning matters, even when they can't articulate why.

Their reasoning revealed something I hadn't fully considered: the judge as information processor whose "cognitive load" might be optimized through disclosure. This framing treats judicial decision-making almost like a computational problem, which is both troubling and insightful. It suggests they see legal reasoning as potentially algorithmic while still recognizing human judges need different contextual cues than they might realize.

The fragment about law firms' changing role hints at deeper anxieties about professional identity that they weren't ready to fully articulate. Their migration from abstract questions about truth ("THE MIRROR") to concrete procedural concerns ("THE MOTION") reveals a cohort that processes philosophical disruption through practical frameworks—perhaps because those feel more manageable.

This student seems to conceptualize truth as emergent from process rather than authorial intent, yet they're still grappling with when and why human agency matters in that process. They're not rejecting AI assistance but trying to locate the boundaries where disclosure becomes ethically necessary—a more nuanced position than outright acceptance or resistance.
