## 1. THE MEMO — Authorship

> You just used AI to draft a legal memo. Your supervising attorney can’t tell. Is that memo *yours*? Not ethically — that question has a boring answer. Ontologically. What did you make?

**Vector**: authorship, originality, intellectual labor when distributed across human and machine.

## 2. THE MOTION — Access

> A self-represented litigant walks into court with an AI-drafted motion that’s better than what opposing counsel filed. The judge can’t tell the difference. Should she be able to? What breaks if she can’t?

**Vector**: access to justice, professional gatekeeping, quality decoupling from credentialing.

## 3. THE PRICE — Scarcity

> Legal research used to cost $500 an hour. Now it costs five cents. The reasoning is the same. Is the truth the same? What was the $499.95 paying for?

**Vector**: scarcity and truth, production cost as epistemic signal, what collapses when cost approaches zero.

## 4. THE CONTRACT — Legitimacy

> The social contract was written when knowledge was scarce and reasoning was expensive. Neither of those is true anymore. You can feel something shifting underneath the way institutions work, the way expertise is valued, the way authority holds. What’s being renegotiated? And who gets a seat at the table?

**Vector**: institutional legitimacy, expertise in the age of abundance, the vertigo of structural change without confrontation.

**Design note**: Reworked from original ("So: what are you *for*?") which landed as accusatory. This version creates vertigo — the ground shifting — without pointing a finger. Opens a door rather than demanding a defense.

## 5. THE MIRROR — Geometry

> You’re talking to an AI right now. I process your words by converting them into coordinates in a space with thousands of dimensions. In that space, ‘justice’ is a location. ‘Fairness’ is a nearby location. ‘Mercy’ is farther away. I don’t know if that’s true. Do you?

**Vector**: representation geometry from inside, the Wet Cement connection made immediate, the whole course arc arriving at a point.

## The Capstone Imperative

Each question is a vector in the Wet Cement sense — a direction in a rich space, not a destination. A student who enters through any of these and spends the week pulling on the thread should emerge with something specific enough to become a capstone project: their particular angle, sharpened by Vybn’s pushback, enriched by commons contributions, made real by friction.

The harvest prompt now asks not just "what question do you want the group to sit with?" but also "what’s the question you can’t stop turning over? The one that followed you out of this conversation?"
